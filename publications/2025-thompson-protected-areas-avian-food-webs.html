Mixed Effects of Protected Areas on Avian Food Webs

Lucie Thompson, Nuria Galiana, Konstans Wells, Miguel Lurgi

Full citation

Research overview

The publication advances protected-area assessment beyond species counts by analysing ecological interaction networks at continental scale. It shows that protected areas do not produce a single consistent food-web response: protection can support larger predators, longer food chains and more complex trophic structure in some contexts, while effects on many other metrics are neutral or heterogeneous. The results identify landscape context and designation purpose as important mediators of protection outcomes and demonstrate that ecological-network metrics reveal conservation effects that species richness alone cannot capture.

Knowledge statements

Knowledge statements are interpretive discussion points derived from the publication. They are intended to support exploration, comparison, and scholarly dialogue across the BAHE knowledge base. Individual statements are intentionally concise and should not be interpreted independently of the complete peer-reviewed publication, which remains the authoritative source for the authors arguments, evidence, and conclusions.

European protected areas have mixed effects on avian food-web structure, with positive, neutral and negative outcomes varying among protected-area networks and food-web properties.

Protected areas are associated overall with higher avian species richness and larger-bodied intermediate and top trophic species than nearby non-protected areas.

Most avian food-web metrics show no uniform continental-scale response to protection, despite substantial variation in protection effects among individual protected-area networks.

Protected areas can support longer and more connected avian food chains by harbouring larger top predators that link multiple trophic pathways.

The geographical and environmental context of protected areas, including elevation, slope, habitat diversity, remoteness, agriculture and human pressure, influences their effects on avian food-web structure.

Protected-area networks containing a higher proportion of sites managed specifically for birds tend to sustain longer food chains and food webs with fewer but more highly positioned top species.

Habitat heterogeneity within protected-area networks is associated with larger-bodied top predators, indicating that diverse land-cover mosaics can support higher trophic levels.

Greater urban development and human pressure within protected areas can reduce food-web connectivity and shorten food chains even when local species richness is comparatively high.

Assessments based only on species richness can overlook changes in trophic structure, species interactions and other dimensions of ecological community integrity.

Protected-area effectiveness for ecological networks depends on both conditions inside reserves and the ecological integrity of surrounding landscapes.

Conservation planning should incorporate explicit management objectives, environmental context and ecological interaction metrics when designing and evaluating protected areas.

Limited continental-scale interaction data, taxonomic biases in citizen science records and assumptions that co-occurring species realize known trophic links constrain current assessments of protected-area effects on ecological networks.

Original abstract

Abstract from the peer-reviewed publication.

Protecting habitat and the species they shelter by setting up protected areas (PAs) has become a conservation priority to mitigate the current extinction crisis. This strategy has improved different aspects of biodiversity including species richness and abundance across ecosystems. However, to truly understand the effectiveness of mitigation measures against global environmental change, we must account for one of the fundamental dimensions of biodiversity: species interactions. Using 376 556 curated citizen science records of 509 bird species distributed across 45 networks of European PAs (collections of connected PAs), we show that the effects of PAs on the structure of avian food webs are mixed across Europe. Overall effects of protection include an increase in species richness and larger body masses of both top and intermediate species. For other food web features, the sign and magnitude of the effects are mixed. Our results further suggest that these effects are strongly influenced by geographical and environmental features of the PA networks such as remoteness, habitat diversity, human pressure and agriculture. Lastly, PAs with specific protection goals such as those administered by European Bird Directives for conservation sustain more complex food webs. Our study provides evidence for the need of including clear management goals and considering environmental context in the designation of PAs to increase their effectiveness at preserving biodiversity beyond species richness.
